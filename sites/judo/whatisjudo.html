The art of Kodokan Judo was derived from the bujutsu (ancient martial or warlike) arts of feudal Japan. Predominant among these arts was that of jujitsu. The founder of Kodokan Judo (judo for short) was Dr. Jigoro Kano, a Rhodes Scholar, and a noted educator in Japan, who took the art of judo and used his influence to establish it as the basis for a revitalized physical education program in Japanese schools. This start was the instrument that put judo on the world stage and later into the Olympic Games. Today, the sport and art of judo is practiced in over 92 countries throughout the world.

Since Jujutsu schools were in a state of disrepute by the members of Japanese society for being too violent, many of the jujutsu schools came together. Kano's judo was an amalgamation of some of the more popular jujutsu school's of Japan. Prominent among these were the Tenshin Shinyo Ryu and the Kito Ryu schools. When asked why the term judo was used instead of jujutsu, Kano replied "what I teach is not simply jujutsu.  Of course I teach jujutsu, but it is upon 'do' (way or principle)." In addition to encompassing the best of many jujutsu arts, Kano's judo also reflected a broader range of techniques.

Judo still encompasses the five elements of defense as used in old jujutsu: throwing, grappling, joint locks, strangulation and striking (striking is only practiced in kata or formal forms, and not in competition). The old methods have been refined and modified to take in sport as well as methods of fighting which reflect modern society.  In 1964, judo was officially accepted as a sport in the modern Olympics, and has participated in every Olympics since.
