Reasons for Extinction

2000 years ago…

The story of the Thylacine is a sad one.  Many things contributed to their demise.  We do know that it used to be widespread in New Guinea about 7,000 years ago and has probably been extinct there for 2,000 years.  This has been attributed to wild dogs which competed for the same niche.

Before the Aborigines arrived in Australia about 7,000 years ago, the Thylacine was at the top of the food chain.  With the Aborigines' arrival came their pet dogs (Dingos).  As with the Wild dogs of New Guinea, the dingo was a more effective hunter and breeder.  Thylacines where quickly diminished from the mainland.  The last sign of one on mainland Australia is a fossil found in West Australia, which is 3,100 years old.

The Thylacine found refuge on the island of Tasmania, which was once connected to the continent by a land bridge, and became the only place on Earth where Thylacines were found.  As the dingo did not occur in Tasmania, the Thylacine was able to exist relatively undisturbed until European occupation.

The arrival of the Europeans

The Europeans settled in mainland Australia in 1803.  Upon their arrival the settlers noticed the continent's bounty of strange animals.  Reports of a tiger like animal where starting to be taken notice of.  In 1808 the first Thylacine was shot and that same year George Harris, Deputy Surveyor of Van Diemen's Land (Now known as Tasmania), published the first scientific description.

The Thylacine preyed on chickens and sheep belonging to the new settlers.  As a consequence many Thylacines were shot

by land holders.  It didn't take long for the conflicts with the farmers to be dealt with more seriously.  By 1836 the Van Diemen's Land Company was employing full-time Thylacine trappers and by 1840 rewards of ten shilling each were paid for scalps.

In 1863, when the Thylacines were under no threat, John Gould made the following observation:  When the comparatively small island of Tasmania becomes more densely populated, and its primitive forests are intersected with roads from the eastern to the western coast of Tasmania, the numbers of this singular animal will speedily diminish, extermination will have its full sway, and it will then, like the Wolf in England and Scotland, be recorded as an animal of the past.

The Government Intervenes

The Tasmanian Government came into the business in 1888 and introduced a bounty scheme from 1888-1908, paying £1 (=$2) for an adult and 10 shillings (=$1) for pups.  The reward for adults was the equivalent to half the weekly wage in 1888.  The number of bounties paid by the government totaled 2063.  Skins were exported to be made into waistcoats.  However, more deaths were probably caused by the Dingo, Aborigines and vagabonds.

Bounties on the animal were officially ended in 1909, but the Thylacine was still being hunted down as late as 1930.  Europeans changed the habitat of the Thylacine and that of its prey when they cleared parts of the land for farming and logging purposes.  The construction of dams flooded Thylacine habitat.  This, combined with the bounty scheme and a possible disease1, drastically reduced the numbers of Thylacines until, around 1920, there were only a few left2.  This was more pressure on the population than it was able to withstand.  Population numbers had decreased to below the number required for survival of the species.
In 1929 the Thylacine reached the verge of extinction and was protected during that December which was thought to be their main breeding time.  There was no success in breeding them in captivity despite the large number that went through zoos around the world.

In 1930 at Mawbanna in the far north-west corner of Tasmania, a hunter named Wilf Batty made the last know kill of a Thylacine in the wild.

Benjamin

In 1933, the last known specimen was captured and sold to Beaumauris Zoo in Hobart, where it was named Benjamin.

In 1936 a law was passed declaring Thylacines as a protected species, but only two months later at 6pm on September 6th, 1936, Benjamin died in Hobart Zoo.  The Thylacine was declared officially extinct (There was no success in breeding them in captivity despite the large number that went through zoos around the world).  Less than fifty years had passed since the Tasmanian Government placed the £1 bounty on their scalps.

"With its passing, a whole family of marsupials which had existed for more than 10 million years became extinct." (McNamara)

Some more info

A reason for the Tasmanian Tiger becoming extinct in Tasmania could be that there was little public pressure to try to preserve them.  Only one man seemed to care, Mr T Flynn who suggested that some be put on an island to re-populate, but nothing was done.  Since then, DNA testing has proved that the Thylacine is the only modern member of its family.  The Thylacine has been officially declared extinct under the Tasmanian Threatened Species Protection Act of 1995.

Today the only remains of the Tasmanian Tiger apart from supposed sightings are photos, paintings and short black and white films in museums, and one adult and pouch young preserved in alcohol.  Interestingly, all the photographs of live Thylacines are of captive animals; there are no photographs of the animals in the wild.

The Thylacine is not mourned as a lost species like the extinct dodo or the European bison, not nurtured and cherished like the rare and extremely endangered pandas or Californian condors.  Being so dog-like it has a natural appeal to human emotions and this does not allow us to easily write off the Thylacine.


1  In 1910 an epidemic rather like distemper, and possibly brought by domestic dogs, may have reduced the already dangerously low population.

2  Between 1888-1914 at least 2,268 Thylacines are known to have been killed and turned in.
