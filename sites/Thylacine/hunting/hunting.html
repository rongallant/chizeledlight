Hunting

The Thylacine was a carnivore, and its prey consisted mostly of kangaroos, pademelons, wallabies, small mammals and birds.  The animal also preyed on domestic sheep introduced when Europeans settled in Tasmania.  The sheep were easy targets, and the Thylacine was easily blamed for any sheep losses.  The Thylacine was the largest carnivorous marsupial during the last 20,000 years, so it played an important role as the top predator in its food chain.

The Thylacine preferred to hunt at dusk, night or dawn.  Aided by its remarkably keen sense of smell, its hunting range could cover an enormous distance, anything up to eighty square kilometers.  Thylacines would usually hunt alone or in pairs, but never in packs.  Upon picking up the scent of a wallaby or some other intended victim, the Thylacine would follow its prey at a leisurely trot as it was incapable of any great speed.  Slowly, but with great persistence, it gradually wore down its prey until it showed signs of exhaustion, and finally made the kill in a short swift rush.  The Thylacine was not a fast runner due to its short hind legs and stiff kangaroo-like tail, which made manoeuvring difficult at high speeds, especially in forest areas.

The Thylacine invariably killed its prey by crushing the skull with their powerful jaws.  In devouring its prey, the Thylacine ate only selected parts.  It was primarily a blood feeder, sucking blood from the severed jugular vein of its kill.  It enjoyed the vascular parts of its prey such as the throat, nasal tissue, kidney and liver fat.  It is believed that Thylacines ate only freshly killed meat and never returned to a kill.  This was important because it meant that they could not be poisoned by settlers who baiting carcasses.
